CHANGE BLINDNESS

 Change  blindness also happens when we focus our attention on 

the scene p.eg.  while witnessing crimes

 A experiment by Nelson et  al. (2011)  was  conducted under  the 

assumption  that  better  identification accuracy  is achieved  when 
the crime  seriousness  was  high. 
 They showed their participants videos of an actor stealing either $5 or 

$500 dollar. 

 In one experimental condition no change happened, in the other 

condition the first actor was swapped with another when turning around 
a corner. 

 In the actor change condition only 4.5% of participants noticed the 

change in actors.

 % of participants correctly identified the first actor as the thief. The 

study also showed a high right of misidentification, in 58% of the actor 
change cases participants identified the second actor as the thief.

 Innocent  bystanders  are  more  likely to be  mistaken  for  the 
criminal if they  are  in a related  location or action sequence.

UNCONSCIOUS TRANSFERENCE

 Unconscious transference  is the phenomenon  of eyewitness 

misidentification by  accusing  a familiar,  but  innocent person. 

 The eyewitness  usually  does  not remember  previously  meeting 
the wrongly  accused,  it is suf ficient  if the  eyewitness  has  been 
exposed  to the accused  prior or during  the crime. 

 Real  life examples:

 A sales person at a railroad station was robbed. When asked to identify 
the robber in a police lineup, the agent accused a sailor. Fortunately for 
him the sailor could provide an alibi that was irrefutable. An 
investigation later found out that the sailor had previously bought tickets 
from the ticket agent.

 The case of psychologist D. Thomson further illustrates that the 

eyewitness does not necessarily have to have met the accused in person. 
Thomson was accused of rape by the female victim, who had been forced 
to engage in sexual activities in her home. Thomson was able to evade 
prosecution, stating that, at the time of the rape, he had participated in 
a televised discussion of, ironically, eyewitness memory. It turned out, 
that the victim had been watching this exact discussion before she was 
raped and the T V had been running in the background.

IMPLICATIONS

 Emotion or confidence  do not equate  accuracy!
 Many  studies  have  looked at  whether  or not jurors know about 
the psychological factors  involved  in eyewitness  identification. 
 A study of over 1,000 citizens in Washington, DC has found that many 

people hold wrong believes over the accuracy of memory. 

 In New Jersey jurors are now educated about memory processes  and the 

possibility of distorted memories.

 Eyewitness  evidence  should also follow best -practice  guidelines 

to guarantee  that  they are  admissible. 

 Subconscious  transference  has  been  shown to be  caused  by 

exposure  to  mugshots and  photo-lineups,  therefore  creating  a 
bias in identification.
 In legal proceedings, there needs to be a balance between probative and 

prejudicial value. It is important that the impact of the evidence is not 
greater than its evidential value.
